Saturated & Unsaturated Fat
Study results are clear that saturated fat has little effect on heart disease for most healthy adultsand some
foods high in saturated fat may even be beneficial for heart health.
It’s important to consider the overall composition of foods and how they interact with our bodies rather than
focusing solely on the individual macronutrients they contain. For example, while processed foods and whole
foods may both contain protein, fats, and carbohydrates, these foods have different effects on health. The fats
found in nutrient-dense ingredients such as milk, cheese, and yogurt impact health differently than the
saturated fats found in processed meats or sugary desserts.
And -replacing some of the saturated fat in your diet with unsaturated fat may offer several health benefits.
Not becausesaturated fatis “bad” but because unsaturated fats are particularly healthyand have been
shown to protect against heart disease. Nutritious sources of unsaturated fats include nuts, seeds, fatty fish,
extra-virgin olive oil, and avocados.
So, there doesn’t seem to be any reason for most people to worry about saturated fat. Much more worthy of
your attention islimiting your intake of sugar-sweetened beverages and processed foods, following a well-
balanced diet, and getting plenty of physical activity in your daily routine.

Body Fat
See wiki –adipose tissue
Adipose tissue (also known as body fat or simply fat) is a loose connective tissue composed mostly
of adipocytes. Its main role is to store energy in the form of lipids, although it also cushions and insulates the
body.
Previously treated as being hormonally inert, in recent years adipose tissue has been recognized as a
majorendocrineorgan, as it produces hormones such as leptin, estrogen, resistin, and
cytokines (especially TNFα).
In obesity, adipose tissue is implicated in the chronic release of pro-inflammatory markers known
as adipokines, which are responsible for the development of metabolic syndrome—a constellation of
diseases includingtype 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease and atherosclerosis
The two types of adipose tissue are white adipose tissue (WAT), which stores energy, and brown adipose
tissue (BAT), which generates body heat.

Essential Fatty Acids (EFAs)
Many individuals believe that to lose weight, one must eliminate fat from the diet. Unfortunately, this can be very
harmful to the body as certain types of fats are critical to proper function of the body. These beneficial fats are
known as Essential Fatty Acids (EFAs). They are essential because your body cannot produce them on its own so
they must come from your diet. The two primary EFAs are known as linoleic acid (omega-6) and alpha-linolenic
acid (omega-3).
The omega-6/omega-3 fatty acid ratio is important for health and the prevention of chronic diseases.The ideal ratio
is 4:1, which means thatone gram of omega-3s should be consumed for every 4 grams of omega-6. However, the
typical Western diet provides an omega-6/3 ratio of approximately 20:1 in favor of omega-6, which predisposes to
supraphysiologic inflammatory responses and perpetuates chronic low-grade inflammation. The overconsumption
of linoleic acid, mainly from industrial omega-6 seed oils, and the lack of long-chain omega-3s in the diet creates a
pro-inflammatory, pro-allergic, pro-thrombotic state.Several sources suggest humans evolved on a diet with a ratio
of omega-6 to omega-3 essential fatty acids of approximately 1, whereas in developed countries, the ratio of
omega-6s to omega-3s is closer to 15:1. Combined omega-3-6-9 supplements usually provide each of these fatty
acids in suitable proportions, such as 2-to-1-to-1 for omega-3:6:9.
https://www.webmd.com/diet/features/what-to-know-about-omega-3s-and-fish

Melatonin and Blood Sugar
Melatonin exerts its physiological function through binding to melatonin receptors on target
cells in the eyes, brain and pancreas. The eyes produce their own source of melatonin and
also signal the brain in response to darkness to increase melatonin production and prepare
you for sleep.
Melatonin receptors on the surface of pancreatic beta cells are tasked with regulating blood
sugar levels. Functionally, binding of melatonin to these cells inhibits the secretion of insulin
into the blood (Mulder et al. 2009). Suppression of insulin ensures that, as you sleep, your
brain has an adequate supply of sugar and also helps
keep blood sugar levels stable. Furthermore,
melatonin antagonizes insulin’s role in
regulating satiety and metabolism following
a meal, which is not required at night.

The Circulatory System By the Numbers
The circulatory system is the super-highway of the body.
Men on average have 5 quarts of blood volume, and women have 4
quarts.
Blood leaves and returns to the heart in about 45 seconds.
The average speed of blood through the body is 3-4 mph, traveling
faster through larger veins and arteries, and slowing as it moves
through smaller capillaries.
Adult bodies contain 60,000 miles of blood vessels - more than twice
the circumference of our planet.

## Page 129

Module 13A Lesson 2 2025-11-17
The Heart
The heart is the center of the circulatory system.
It is a pump that creates both suction and propulsion.
The 4 chambers of the heart beat in a coordinated
manner that creates pressure differentials in the
vessels.
This causes vacuums in certain vessels that quickly
bring the blood back to the heart and allow it to move
efficiently against gravity while we are upright.
The heart (both an organ and a muscle) is the only part
of the body that never rests for the entirety of our lives.

Module 13A Lesson 2 2025-11-17
Hormone Function
Hormones are chemical messengers that travel in the blood.
Hormones are responding dynamically to shifting internal and external conditions.
Some hormones also have set patterns they execute across a day (like cortisol) or a month (like
estrogen in women).
1. Hormone is produced by an endocrine gland (hormone-secreting organ)
2. Gland secretes hormone into the blood
3. Hormone travels to the target organ(s)
or tissues
4. Target organs or tissues produce a response
5. Hormones are removed from the blood
6. Hormones are broken down by the liver
Hormones have different response times once
they reach the target organ/ tissue, ranging
from minutes to hours.

Synthetic Hormones
Synthetic Hormones
Hormones manufactured from plant material and other
sources that are similar to hormones produced by the
human body
 Synthetic Estrogen and Progesterone are formulated
using the urine of pregnant horses.
 Testosterone is formulated from man-made chemicals.
 They are similar to hormones produced by the human
body, but they are not identical. They may react
differently with hormone receptors in the body because
they have a different structure.
 They come in standard dosages and use a “one-size-
fits-all” approach.
 They can only be prescribed by a licensed professional.
They are found in:
 Oral Contraceptives

Bioidentical Hormones

Understanding Glands
Gland
an organ that secretes a hormone into the blood stream
or a non-hormone substance for use in the
surrounding local area
The human body has 44 total glands.
3 Types of Glands:
 Endocrine: hormone producing, secretes into blood stream
(e.g. cortisol, sex hormones)
 Exocrine: non-hormone substances secretes through ducts
to local area (e.g. sweat, saliva, breast milk, mucus, tears)
 Mixed: both endocrine & exocrine (liver, pancreas secrete
hormones into blood andsubstances through ducts to local
areas)

Taking animal endocrine glands helps

## Page 161

Module 13A Lesson 2 2025-11-17
Protomorphogens
Protomorphogens
A molecule produced by all cells in animals that
acts as a template for cell structure and function.
Thought to be made in the cell nucleus and regulate
chromosome function, but distinct from RNA and
DNA
Protomorphogens are cell-specific, not species-
specific. Therefore, protomorphogens from an
animal liver, for example, will mimic liver function.
We use the glands of other mammals because they
most closely resemble human endocrine function.
Dr. Royal Lee is the father of protomorphogens
in the modern era. He pioneered their study
and use in the 1950’s.

Oral
Oral Delivery
 Any substance taken by mouth and swallowed
 Substance is processed through the stomach and intestines
 Substance is absorbed through gut epithelial cells to bloodstream
 Digestion of substance takes time, slowest delivery method
Examples of Oral Delivery:
 Tablets/ Capsules
 Soft gels
 Gummies
 Liquids
 Powders mixed into water or other liquid

## Page 195

Module 13A Lesson 6 11/17/2025
Topical & Transdermal
Topical
 Any substance placed on the skin in a localized area
 Substance does not enter the bloodstream
 Example: Vitamin C Face Serum
Transdermal
 Penetrates the skin and enters the bloodstream
 Substance acts throughout the whole body
 Example: Hormone Patches
Both topical and transdermal supplements can be creams, sprays, lotions, liquids,
powders, and patches. The difference in topical and transdermal is the way the
supplement is formulated, to either penetrate the dermis or to stay within the dermis.

Liposomal
Liposome
An artificial vesicle made of a double layer of
phospholipids similar to human cell membrane
used to transport substances into the body.
Liposomes use this similarity to do their job in
two ways:
 They are easily absorbed into the tissues of
the mouth and from there passed to the
blood stream
 They prevent stomach acid from breaking
the substance down and making it unusable
