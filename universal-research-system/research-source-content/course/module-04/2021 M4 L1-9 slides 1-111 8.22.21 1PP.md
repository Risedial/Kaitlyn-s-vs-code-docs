Urinary Indican
The urine indican test (aka Obermeyer test) is an effective screening for improper breakdown of protein; a
critical macro-nutrient found throughout the body –in muscle, bone, skin, hair, and virtually every cell and
tissue. Protein makes up the enzymes that power many chemical reactions and the even the hemoglobin
that carries oxygen in your blood.
At least 10,000 different proteins make you what you are and keep you that way
When there are not enough good bacteria and/or pancreatic juices to properly digest protein for
assimilation through the intestines, it may be acted upon by anaerobic bacteria causing putrefactionand
the production of indole. Most indoles are excreted in the feces, the remainder is re-circulated through
the hepatic portal system (enterohepatic circulation), metabolized by the liver, and excreted as indican in
the urine
Positive Indican = abnormal, can be quantified with the FDN MWP from FLUIDS iQ
Negative Indican = non-clue

Urinary Indican – Improper Protein Breakdown
Indican levels of Low, or higher, may indicate the following: Inadequate dietary protein digestion, intestinal
toxemia and/or an overgrowth of anaerobic bacteria, putrefaction of undigested food in the bowels, various
stomach disorders, such as insufficient hydrochloric acid (HCL), as well as pancreatic insufficiency, especially in
trypsin and chymotrypsin. Indican levels that have reached High to Very High may indicate even greater
insufficiency of HCl, as in hypochlorhydria and/or protease enzyme deficiency. It also may indicate hypomotility of
the upper bowel, liver dysfunction, as well as increases in some common microorganisms such as Salmonella,
Staphylococcus aureus, Candida albicans and other candida species. Inability to digest protein can lead to adverse
effects on glycemic control, and hormone imbalance. See slide 43 for description of ranges.

Urinary Bile Acids – Liver Congestion/Enterohepatic Circulation
Bile acids (BAs) play key roles in many physiological functions, such as cholesterol elimination, fat absorption,
regulation of energy expenditure, as well as glucose and lipid metabolism. They are synthesized in the liver and
then stored in the gallbladder. When the gallbladder contracts, bile acids enter the intestinal lumen and are
(mostly) reabsorbed in the ileum. They are cleared from the portal circulation on the first pass through the liver.
Elevated TBA represents bile acids that were not cleared by the liver (spilled over into bloodstream and excreted
by kidneys) and are used as a screening for liver parenchymal (cellular) damage, an indication of liver dysfunction
(congestion). An increase in TBA may indicate a risk of viral disease, cirrhosis and drug-induced liver injury, as
well as cholestasis. A low level of TBA is suggestive of inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), chronic malabsorption,
persistent diarrhea, or starvation.

8-OHdG Oxidative Stress Indicator
8-OHdG is an oxidized derivative of deoxyguanosine and is one of the major products of DNA oxidation. Its urinary
excretion, is used to assess endogenous oxidative stress and damage in humans after exposure to heavy metals,
tobacco smoke, asbestos fibers and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons. A biomarker of oxidative stress, 8-OHdG is
associated with many disease entities listed in this lesson. Medical oversight may be indicated as elevated levels
are closely associated with high blood pressure and inflammatory conditions such as pancreatitis, and cancer. 8-
OHdG has also been found beneficial for the assessment of exercise-induced oxidative damage. When not
sufficiently balanced by local antioxidant systems, oxidative damage may occur to cellular lipid membranes,
proteins, as well as mitochondrial and nuclear DNA.
