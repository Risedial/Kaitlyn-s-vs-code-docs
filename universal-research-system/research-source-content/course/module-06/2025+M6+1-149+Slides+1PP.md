GI MAP Luminex Technology
Using the Luminex molecular technology, Diagnostic Solutions Laboratory developed assays for
microbes useful in functional medicine. Each target organism was fully validated. Target organisms
were chosen based on the level of confidence of reporting accuracy and which organisms might be
most useful and actionable from a clinical perspective.
Accurate measurement of DNA requires:
 Amplification = making copies of target genes
• Extraction and purification of nucleic acids
• Multiplex PCR (polymerase chain reaction), to amplify many target genes – measures 16s
and 23s regions of ribosomal RNA (rRNA), virulence factors, and viral targets
 Hybridization = matching single-stranded DNA from the targets to the probes in a lock-and-key
manner
• Treated with magnetic beads imbedded with DNA probes, which are segments of DNA that
seek to join with complementary matches and are radioactively labelled for measurement
• Hybridization is the binding of one single-stranded DNA segment to another complementary
piece of DNA

Normal Bacterial Flora - Analysis
Dysbiosis = Imbalance in the microbiome that has a negative impact upon health of the host
 Typically means a decrease in “good” bacteria and an increase in “bad” bacteria
Good bacteria
 Promote mucosal barrier integrity
 Support and balance the immune system
 Support the colonization of other “good” bacteria
 Convert food into other beneficial products
 Produce SCFA which reduce inflammation, increase SIgA and mucin, and protect against toxins
Bad bacteria
 Produce pro-inflammatory LPS, increasing inflammation and gut permeability
 Encourage the overgrowth of other opportunistic, pro-inflammatory bacteria
 Deplete population levels of “good” bacteria
NOTE: Normal flora being out of range is not always a sign of dysbiosis. If commensal bacteria are abnormally
high, consider anti-microbials (even if the remainder of the lab report is unremarkable). If commensal bacteria
are abnormally low, consider “re-seeding” of the gut with a probiotic regimen

Phyla Microbiota
Bacteroidetes phylum – consists of gram-negative bacteria
Firmicutes phylum – consists of gram-positive bacteria
 These phyla dominate the entire GI tract – including nose, mouth, throat and colon; comprise
appx. 90% of the total microbiota
 Diets that promote fat loss, as well as oral glutamine, may lower the F/B ratio
 Lactobacillus spp. and Bacillus spp. can increase Firmicutes levels
 Bifidobacteria spp and Saccharomyces boulardii can increase Bacteroidetes levels
 Elevated Firmicutes/Bacteroidetes ratio suggests microbial imbalance that may be related to
maldigestion, hypochlorhydria, increased fat deposition, insulin resistance, excess
inflammation, excess carbohydrate intake, obesity, and inability to lose weight
Note: It is well established that gram negative bacteria produce Lipopolysaccharides (LPS) which are known to
dysregulate the tight junctions of the mucosal barrier, leading to increased hyperpermeability. However, the
LPS that are produced by the Bacteroidetes phyla are actually anti-inflammatory and protective to the gut lining

Insufficiency Dysbiosis
 Insufficiency Dysbiosis is characterized by an overall pattern of depletion that promotes greater
risk of intestinal infections, hyper-permeability of the gut, and compromised immune function
• Low levels of normal flora – Bacteroides fragilis, Bifidobacterium spp., Enterococcus spp.,
Escherichia spp., and Lactobacillus spp.
• Low levels of Phyla Microbiota – Firmicutes and/or Bacteroidetes
• Correlating intestinal health markers – low or very low levels of Secretory IgA, Anti-gliadin
IgA often elevated, Zonulin sometimes elevated
 Pathogens are often present with this pattern
 Microbes that promote inflammation and autoimmunity can flourish in this environment
 Insufficiency dysbiosis can transform into a combined pattern of Insufficiency/Inflammatory
Dysbiosis

Inflammatory Dysbiosis
 Inflammatory Dysbiosis is characterized by the presence of pro-inflammatory pathogens,
elevated normal flora and opportunistic organisms
• Low to high levels of pathogens – Campylobacter, C. difficile, pathogenic E. coli,
Salmonella, Vibrio cholerae, Yersinia enterocolitica, and Giardia
• High levels of normal flora – Escherichia spp. and Enterobacter spp.
• Moderate to high levels of opportunistic organisms – Morganella spp., Pseudomonas spp.,
Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Citrobacter spp., Citrobacter freundii, Klebsiella pneumoniae,
Proteus spp., Proteus mirabilis, Candida spp., Candida albicans, and protozoan parasites
• Correlating intestinal health markers – low or high levels of Secretory IgA, Calprotectin
often high but sometimes very low, Zonulin may be elevated
 Intestinal hyper-permeability is common with this pattern d/t high levels of pro-inflammatory
LPS produced by gram-negative bacteria from the Proteobacteria phylum
NOTE: Pro-inflammatory bacteria promote LPS, provoke gut immune response, increase IL6, Th17 and TNF-alpha

Digestive Dysfunction Dysbiosis
 Digestive Dysfunction Dysbiosis – when digestive dysfunction alters the microbiome d/t hypochlorhydria,
insufficient bile acids, pancreatic insufficiency, reduced absorption, and/or compromised gastrointestinal
motility
• Pathogens present in low to high levels
• H. pylori often present in moderate to high levels
• Elevation of normal flora - Enterococcus spp., Lactobacillus spp., and Clostridium spp.
• High levels of Phyla Microbiota – Bacteroidetesand/or Firmicutes
• Moderate to high levels of opportunistic bacteria, yeast and parasites – Bacillus spp, Enterococcus
faecalis, Enterococcus faecium, Morganella spp., Staphylococcus spp., Staphylococcus aureus,
Streptococcusspp., Klebsiella pneumoniae, Prevotella copri, Candida spp., Candida albicans, and any of
the protozoan parasites
• Correlating intestinal health markers –Elastase 1 often low to moderately low, steatocrit often elevated
 Common clinical correlations: gas and bloating, abdominal discomfort, heartburn/GERD, constipation,
diarrhea, SIBO, feeling excessively full after a meal, and food sensitivities

H. Pylori background
H. Pylori is a spiral-shaped, gram-negative
bacteria that burrows into the stomach lining. It
may neutralize the stomach acid surrounding it,
compromising host’s ability to digest food and kill
other pathogens coming into the stomach
Certain proteins and genes cause H. pylori to
proliferate or magnify its damaging effects
These are called virulence factors.

Virulence Factors
Adhesion Virulence Factor Certain enzymatic reactions produce
adhesion proteins, which help H. pylori attach to the stomach lining,
increasing odds that the infection will be persistent long enough to
become pathogenic
Inflammatory Virulence Factors As H. pylori adheres and burrows
into the gastric lining, certain DNA coding allows the bacteria to
change the way the body’s signaling pathways talk to each other,
telling anti-inflammatory processes to ignore the damage and stay
inflamed. These virulence factors also can tell the immune system to
ignore H. Pylori, even as it causes cumulative damage to the gastric
lining, This damage can culminate in ulcers or even cancer
