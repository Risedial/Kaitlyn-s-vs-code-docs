Why Test Zonulin?
Zonulin plays a pivotal role in the control of the tight junctions of the small intestine. As
mentioned, increased Zonulin levels are seen in many conditions and diseases associated with
increased intestinal inflammation. Changes in permeability can precede clinical manifestations
by up to a year. For that reason, Zonulin is gaining acceptance as a non-invasive marker of
intestinal wall integrity and developing disorders.
Zonulin also plays an important role in permeability changes in the brain, working as a
gatekeeper, not only in the intestine, but at the blood brain barrier (BBB). This is clearly in
evidence when there is an intake of foods containing α-gliadin or similar proteins. The resulting
high Zonulin levels leads to disassembly of the TJs in the vascular epithelium, permitting many
molecules including toxins, to slip through the BBB, resulting in activation of a cerebral
inflammatory response. Whether this is due to microglial activation, as seen in traumatic brain
injury, or other neurological processes, this area is becoming of great interest for clinicians.

Why test for Histamine?
Histamine is a naturally occurring chemical that plays a key role in modulating the
Immune system, released particularly by mast cells, in response to injury, infection
and STRESS. It can be both pro-inflammatory and anti-inflammatory depending on
the context in which it is released. Histamine can cause vasodilation and increase
vascular permeability, allowing Immune cells to more easily migrate to sites of injury
or infection. Histamine can also activate immune cells and promote a more effective
Immune response. On the other hand, histamine can
also suppress the Immune response. Produced and
stored in mast cells and found in various tissues,
particularly in the skin, respiratory system, and
Digestive tract. We see it elevated on lab reports along
with Zonulin, as Zonulin tries to flush it out of the
lamina propria, eventually leading to leaky gut.
