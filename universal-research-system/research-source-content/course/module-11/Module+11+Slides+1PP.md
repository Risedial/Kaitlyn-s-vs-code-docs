Exercise for Healing- Find your Level
Before beginning an exercise regimen, it is crucial to evaluate your state of
health in order to determine the best approach for your needs.
“Eating better” and “being more active” can lead to health improvements, but
only if done at an appropriate rate.
Many are unsuccessful because they are approaching it from a “restrictive”
and “punishment” manner in which they are eating less calories and jumping
into inappropriately intense bouts of exercise in order to lose weight.
We need to always keep in mind that “losing weight” and “building health” are
not always the same thing.
More importantly, you can’t “diet and exercise” your way out of Metabolic Chaos®!

Exercise Programming
Most clients will benefit from an exercise regimen that includes a varietyof activities and intensities.
Their program should help achieve their health and fitness goals but fit within current HPA Axis status:
If a client is in the “exhaustive phase” of HPA axis dysfunction and they are wanting an exercise regimen
that will help them burn fat and build muscle, they need to understand that the volume of exercise that
may be required will likely impair their adrenal glands from getting the break they so desperately need.
Similarly, a client with a GI infection and in the acute phase of HPA axis dysfunction may need to
minimize bouts of intense exercise as elevated cortisol can further weaken the gut’s defense
mechanisms, such as SIgA levels.
Be sure to work with the client on prioritizing their health and fitness goals to avoid such conflicts.
Healing must take place BEFORE fitness goals can best be reached.
You can’t “diet and exercise” your way out of Metabolic Chaos®!

Exercise Programming
Exercise programs should be well rounded to allow for specific adaptationsto various stimuli that include
Increased proprioception-improved eye hand coordination, body awareness, balance, agility, stabilization.
Increased range of motion-being able to take a joint through it’s full range of motion with improved mobility
and flexibility, thus allowing healthier joint mechanics and structural integrity.
Increased cardiovascular endurance-improved oxygen transport/delivery, mitochondrial function and density
in slow twitch muscle fibers, cardiac muscle strength.
Increased muscular endurance-improved work capacity for fast twitch muscle fibers, greater creatineand
glycogen storage capacity.
Increased strength-improved sarcomere density (contractile units), greater neural motor control, increased
muscle fiber quantity.

Activities to Include: HIIT
High Intensity Interval Training (HIIT). Primarily works the glycolyticsystems by performing one or more
movements at a high degree of intensity to raise the heart rate as high as possible. Example workouts:
30 second sprints with 15 seconds rest in between intervals for
10 rounds (can be performed on elliptical, treadmill)
Plyometric circuit 1 minute work/ 20 seconds rest for 5 rounds
 Squat jump
 Jumping Jack
 Burpee
 Mountain climbers
HIIT training can be done after resistance training to capitalize
on the catecholamine (stress response) surge that the resistance training stimulates to further enhance
fat burning. HIIT can also be a standalone workout. Keep in mind to strive for a high heart rate, a sense
of exertion and feeling out of breath, a burn in the muscles, a high body temperature with plenty of
sweating. https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28447186

Activities to Include: Cardio
Primarily works the lipolytic (aerobic) energy system by performing movement for long duration at
moderate to low intensity. Example Activities: walking, jogging, rowing, biking for 20+ minutes.
Cardio can be completed after resistance training, HIIT, or
as a stand alone workout. Generally cardiovascular
exercise lasts anywhere from 20 minutes to multiple hours
depending on the goal. Cardio can be further enhanced by
training in target heart rate zones to improve specific levels
of endurance. Aerobic (oxygen sufficient) exercise can
increase muscular and cardiovascular endurance while
ramping up fat utilization during the workout.

Activities to Include: Resistance Training
Any movement done against resistance. Includes bodyweight calisthenics or any weighted movements
such as lifting weights, pushing a sled, carrying a load etc.
Multi joint, complex movements should be prioritized over more isolated, single joint movements. Ie. Squats
(hip and knee flexion/extension) should be prioritized over curls (elbow flexion/extension) as the squat works
more muscles and larger muscles, thus yielding a greater functional response and result.
Some common exercises for each of the major muscle groups can include-
Hips/Legs: Squat, deadlift, lunge, step up, hip thrusts
Chest: Bench press, DB press, chest fly, push ups, dips
Back: pull up, cable row, lat pull, bent row, deadlift
Shoulders: Military Press, handstand push up, frontal raises,
shrugs, dips
Core: plank, side plank, flutter kicks, hanging leg/knee raises,
crunches, sit ups

Resistance Training
Sets and rep ranges can vary based on the desired outcome.
Low reps with high resistance elicits greater strength and neural benefits (3 sets of 3-5 reps)
Moderate reps with moderate resistance yields the greatest gains in lean mass (4 sets of 8-10 reps)
High volume with low resistance yields the greatest gains in muscular endurance. (5 sets of 15+ reps)
A varietyof set and rep schemes should be used depending on the phase of training and desired outcome.
Regardless of the set and rep range, muscular failure should be strived for. Without pushing yourself, your body
will have no reason to strengthen itself.
Strength training prevents insulin resistance: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27487746

Activities to Include: Myofascial Release
A form of exercise/warm-up dedicated to releasing muscular adhesions or “knots” from micro-
spasms in muscle tissue, which cause functional shortening of the muscle. Includes foam rolling,
massage and other soft tissue work. Applying mechanical pressure to the knot tells the nervous
system to “stop contracting”, thus releasing the knot or micro-spasm. Massage and self myo-fascial
release can alleviate muscle pain while promoting proper circulation of both blood and lymph fluid.

Activities to Include: Active Recovery
Active Recovery is less strict and pertains to any activity that is lower in intensity and
duration to promote blood flow, circulation, muscle activation and range of motion,
while avoiding excess strain. This includes shorter/lighter resistance workouts, walking,
yoga, or any light activity that the individual deems appropriate for their recovery.

Nutrition for Exercise
Any time a client changes activity level, they may also need to consider adjusting their nutritional intaketo
correspond with their activity. Exercise can change our caloric and nutrient needs as well as modifying our
biochemistry post workout.
A few key changes that occur during and post workout are:
 insulin sensitivity is greatly increased
 catecholamines and cortisol spike
 testosterone temporarily increases
 growth hormone and IGF increase
 inflammatory cytokines rise
We can adjust our nutritional intake to take advantage of some
of these effects in order to promote recovery, muscle growth,
and fat loss. Breakfast?

Nutrition Strategies to Promote Recovery
During the post exercise window we can promote
recoveryand muscle growth by:
 consuming protein to increase protein synthesis in
the ribosomes of our cells. Can use protein powders
(no soy).
 consuming carbohydrates to raise insulin, suppress
cortisol, replenish muscle glycogen, and stimulate
anabolic repair. Can use carbohydrate powders.
 taking phosphatidylserine orally or topically to blunt
the cortisol response.
 drinking amino acids (bcaa and/or eaa) during
workout to decrease muscle breakdown.
 consuming a regular meal within 1-2 hours

increase carbohydrate intake slowly to avoid
