Determining Correct Macronutrient Ratios
Educate your client on how to pay attention to their body
• Use the Diet Check Record sheet (DCRs).
• Consider each meal in terms of overall metabolic effect
• After a meal, did what they eat provide:
• a sense of satiety (satisfaction until the next meal, and not craving anything)?
• good solid energy (no slumps, and not nervous energy)?
• a sense of wellbeing (happy, and not irritated)?
• If not, then adjust their macronutrient ratios:
• If they ate a high-carb/low-protein meal, try adding a lot more protein and fat.
• If they ate a high-protein/low-carb meal, try adding a lot more carbohydrate.
• And remember, everymeal must contain all fuel types (carb, protein, andfat)
The overall metabolic effect is largely dictated by the macronutrient ratios

## Page 62

Carbohydrate Digestion
In the mouth, starch is broken down into smaller chains of sugars (oligosaccharides)
by the enzyme salivary amylase
In the stomach, we know HCl (hydrochloric acid) breaks down cell walls of plant-based foods
In the small intestine, oligosaccharides are further broken down into disaccharides (2 molecules of sugar) by
pancreatic amylase, disaccharides are broken down into simple sugars (monosaccharides) by brush border
enzymes (released by the microvilli):
• Lactasecatabolizes lactose to glucose and galactose
• Sucrasecatabolizes sucrose to glucose and fructose
• Maltasecatabolizes maltose to two units of glucose
When we have cases of gut dysfunction or any other hidden stressor that is interfering with optimal digestion,
having an understanding of the role of HCl and digestive enzymes helps us understand why we may need to
support digestion with supplements until those things are addressed.

Glucose
When blood glucose levels rise, the pancreas secretes insulin, which signals the body’s cells to take up

Blood Sugar Balance
If blood sugar levels are too low, the person may feel weak and dizzy (hypoglycemia); too high, and the
person may experience drowsiness and vomiting (hyperglycemia). To maintain homeostasis, the body strives to
maintain balanced levels of blood sugar.
• When blood glucose levels drop, the pancreas halts its secretion of insulin and releases glucagon, a
hormone that signals the liver to convert glycogen to glucose (glycogenolysis) and release it into the
bloodstream. (If there are no glycogen stores, the liver synthesizes glucose from fats and amino
acids –gluconeogenesis.)
• When blood glucose levels rise, the pancreas halts its secretion of glucagon and releases insulin, a
hormone that signals the body cells and the liver to take up glucose from the blood.
• The liver converts the glucose to glycogen (glycogenesis) for storage. Blood glucose levels therefore drop.
• When levels are no longer high, insulin release halts. (It’s a cyclic process.)

for failure to provide regulation from food. Chronic hormonal compensation leads
to adrenal (HPA) dysfunction and alone can cause dysfunction in any process
regulated by the adrenals (HPA)” ~ Bill Wolcott

Fat Absorption and Metabolism
In the small intestine
• Within the lumen, monoglycerides and fatty acids associate with bile salts and phospholipids to form
micelles, which travel to the surface of the enterocytes (the cells lining the small intestine) and are
small enough to fall between the microvilli.
• The monoglycerides and fatty acids are then released from the micelles and absorbed (diffuse
across
the plasma membrane of the enterocyte).
• Within the enterocytes, monoglycerides and fatty acids are resynthesized into triglycerides.
• The triglycerides are packaged, along with cholesterol and fat-soluble vitamins, into
chylomicrons (lipoproteins). The chylomicrons are absorbed into the lacteals of the villi.
Within the lymphatic vessels, the chylomicrons travel via the lymph to the vena cava
and then to the heart (the blood supply). Via the capillaries, chylomicrons deliver the triglycerides
to the body's cells. The triglycerides are broken down (by an enzyme found in capillary
endothelial cells, lipoprotein lipase) into monoglycerides and fatty acids, which then pass
through the capillary wall to be taken up by the cells.

Cholesterol
Cholesterol is a waxy substance found in most body tissues, with high amounts in the liver, brain, nervous
tissue, and blood. Cholesterol is primarily produced by the body (20% in the liver, and 80% in other tissues)
from fragments of carbohydrate, protein, and fat.
• Onlysmall amounts come from the diet (meats, animal fats, dairy products, and egg yolks). Carried
from the liver to the body cells by LDL(low density lipoprotein). In the cell, enzymes liberate the
cholesterol so it can be used by the cell for various functions:
• As a structural component of the cell membrane
• To make steroid hormones (sex hormones and adrenal hormones)
• To make vitamin D (necessary for bone health, immunity, and mood)
• To make bile salts (essential for fat assimilation)
Cholesterol is carried to the liver by HDL(high density lipoprotein) where it can be excreted in the bile (and is re-
circulated). High levels of LDL (recognized as bad) indicates lots of fats are being distributed to the cells. High
HDL (recognized as good) indicates lots of fats are being taken away from the cells.

A general rule of thumb for the amount of pure water per day to maintain hydration is as follows:
• US Standard --½ bodyweight in ounces

Oxalic acid (naturally occurring in rhubarb, spinach, sweet potato, beetroot, cacao) forms oxalates

limiting absorption of these minerals
Discarding the cooking water can reduce oxalic acid levels

Fermentation
Fermented foods are probiotic, containing many species of beneficial bacteria.
Fermentation is used by many ancient cultures to preserve food, promote good digestion, and improve health.
• The fermentation process converts the sugars and
starches to organic acids (e.g. lactic acid). Lactic acid
preserves the food by inhibiting putrefying bacteria.
• Lactic acid promotes a favorably acidic pH in the
intestines that promotes healthy intestinal flora,
inhibiting the growth of some undesirable bacteria.
• Lactic acid can potentially enhance mineral absorption,
especially iron, zinc, calcium, copper, magnesium, and
manganese.

Cooking and Nutrient Availability
Cooking increasesbioavailability of many nutrients by breaking down cellulose, making the nutrients
more accessible to the body’s digestive enzymes.
• Bioavailability of beta-carotene,iron, and calcium increases when foods are cooked
compared with when they are raw. Levels of lycopene (an antioxidant) in tomatoes
increases when they are cooked
Cooking can also result in nutrient losses–varies depending on temperature, duration, and
method of cooking.
Cooking Temperature
• Water-soluble vitamins are sensitive to heat --between 40% and 75% of B
vitamins are destroyed during cooking
Cooking Duration
• More vitamins are lost with increased cooking time --tomatoes cooked at
190.4°F(88°C) lose 29% of vitamin C when cooked for 30 minutes versus 10%
when cooked for 2 minutes

Cooking and Nutrient Availability, cont’d
Cooking in Water
• Minerals are not destroyed by heat, but mostare water-soluble, so these
along with water-soluble vitamins can be lost when discarding cooking water.
• To ensure the least minerals are lost, reduce the surface contact with the water
by cutting foods into big chunks instead of small, and whenever possible consume
the cooking water
Cooking in Aluminum Cookware
• Aluminum is a toxic metal and can be leached into the cooking water.
• Aluminum can inhibit the absorption of vitamins B1, B3, C, and E, selenium,
sodium, phosphorus, potassium, and zinc.
• Cooking in aluminum should be avoided. Good choices are enamel, cast iron,
stainless steel, or oven-safe glass
Pressure Cooking
• Has been shown to preserve enzymes and important nutrients by reducing cooking times
