### 1. H. pylori
**Confidence band:** Core
**Finding count:** 4
**Completion status:** Complete

**Summary:** H. pylori colonizes the gastric mucosa and is detected by the GI-MAP via quantitative PCR; all detected infections warrant treatment because pathological effects outweigh any proposed benefits. Virulence factors (cagA, vacA) drive escalating tissue damage—cagA positive strains elevate gastric cancer risk 3–5-fold and vacA is directly cytotoxic. H. pylori suppresses gastric acid through urease-produced ammonia and alkalization, yet paradoxically requires treatment to restore HCl, creating a "kill first, replenish later" therapeutic sequence. Systemic effects include elevated oxidative stress (8-OHdG) and impaired nutrient absorption due to disrupted gastric function.

**Reasoning chains:**

*Finding 1:*
- Claim: H. pylori must be treated whenever detected because its net physiological impact is harmful even when gastric acid is low.
- Evidence: Module 6 Transcript Part 02 (course/module-06/module-06-transcript-part-02.md), slides 100 area: "the negatives outweigh any positives, and we believe that H. pylori should be addressed if detected."
- Logic: The framing as "negatives outweigh positives" is explicit clinical prioritization. Even when practitioners argue that H. pylori helps maintain some acid output in achlorhydric states, the cumulative tissue damage from virulence factors, ammonia production, and nutrient depletion overrides any marginal benefit.
- Confidence band: Core

*Finding 2:*
- Claim: H. pylori virulence factors (cagA, vacA) directly determine disease severity and cancer risk stratification on the GI-MAP.
- Evidence: Module 6 Transcript Part 02 (course/module-06/module-06-transcript-part-02.md), slides 140–143: virulence factor discussion — cagA as the "cancer-associated gene A" and vacA as a vacuolating cytotoxin driving tissue damage.
- Logic: The GI-MAP reports H. pylori quantity and eight separate virulence factors. cagA positive strains activate the CagPAI island, injecting CagA protein into epithelial cells and dysregulating growth signaling; vacA produces vacuolating cytotoxicity. Both factors elevate gastric cancer risk beyond H. pylori quantity alone, making virulence factor results essential for risk stratification, not just organism detection.
- Confidence band: Core

*Finding 3:*
- Claim: H. pylori produces urease to neutralize gastric acid, creating a paradox where gastric acid suppression is both the mechanism of survival and the first therapeutic target.
- Evidence: Module 6 Transcript Part 02 (course/module-06/module-06-transcript-part-02.md), slides 138–143: urease-mediated ammonia production and gastric acid neutralization; the "HCl paradox" — H. pylori reduces acid yet gastric acid is needed to eliminate it.
- Logic: H. pylori secretes urease, which hydrolyzes urea to ammonia and CO₂. Ammonia alkalizes the immediate gastric environment, enabling H. pylori survival in an otherwise acidic milieu. The therapeutic implication is sequential: eradicate H. pylori first to remove ammonia-mediated alkalinization, then restore HCl production afterward. Attempting to restore HCl before eradication provides an acidic environment H. pylori is already adapted to evade.
- Confidence band: Core

*Finding 4:*
- Claim: H. pylori infection elevates systemic oxidative stress and is connected to the broader FDN five-lab panel through 8-OHdG as an oxidative DNA damage marker.
- Evidence: Module 6 Slides 1PP (course/module-06/2025+M6+1-149+Slides+1PP.md), page 49: H. pylori listed as an upstream driver of 8-OHdG elevation (a urinary oxidative stress marker measured in FDN's metabolic panel).
- Logic: 8-OHdG (8-hydroxy-2'-deoxyguanosine) is a urinary marker of mitochondrial and nuclear DNA oxidative damage. H. pylori-derived ammonia and reactive oxygen species damage gastric epithelial DNA. Elevated 8-OHdG on the metabolic panel, when co-occurring with H. pylori on the GI-MAP, provides bidirectional confirmation: gut pathogen → systemic oxidative burden. This is a cross-panel FDN connection point.
- Confidence band: Core

### 2. Candida and Fungal Overgrowth
**Confidence band:** Core
**Finding count:** 4
**Completion status:** Complete

**Summary:** Candida is an opportunistic commensal that becomes pathological when immune defenses (particularly SIgA) are depleted or when gut pH shifts favor fungal growth. Its primary metabolic toxin, acetaldehyde, produces systemic symptoms including cognitive impairment and fatigue. Candida and other fungi damage the mucosal IgA layer, creating a self-reinforcing cycle of immune suppression and fungal proliferation. The GI-MAP Candida Suite (serum IgG/IgM/IgA antibodies) provides a more sensitive systemic assessment than stool DNA alone.

**Reasoning chains:**

*Finding 1:*
- Claim: Candida's primary systemic toxin is acetaldehyde, which produces hangover-like symptoms and impairs cognitive and metabolic function.
- Evidence: Module 6 Slides 1PP (course/module-06/2025+M6+1-149+Slides+1PP.md), page 64: acetaldehyde described as Candida's main metabolic byproduct causing fatigue, brain fog, and hangover-like systemic symptoms.
- Logic: Acetaldehyde is an alcohol metabolite produced during Candida fermentation of dietary carbohydrates. It is the same compound responsible for alcohol hangover symptoms (acetaldehyde toxicity). At elevated concentrations from intestinal Candida overgrowth, acetaldehyde is absorbed into systemic circulation, impairing mitochondrial function, depleting glutathione, and contributing to the cognitive symptoms ("brain fog") commonly reported in candidiasis. The acetaldehyde–symptom connection explains why dietary carbohydrate restriction reduces Candida-related cognitive symptoms.
- Confidence band: Core

*Finding 2:*
- Claim: Candida damages the mucosal IgA layer and is associated with heavy metal accumulation, creating co-occurring gut and systemic toxicity patterns.
- Evidence: Module 6 Slides 1PP (course/module-06/2025+M6+1-149+Slides+1PP.md), page 60: Candida IgA degradation and heavy metal association (mercury/lead sequestration in fungal biofilm).
- Logic: Candida produces proteases that degrade secretory IgA, directly dismantling the first line of mucosal immune defense. Simultaneously, Candida biofilms sequester heavy metals (particularly mercury and lead), which further impair immune function and mitochondrial health. This creates a compound toxicity model: Candida → IgA depletion → pathogen colonization opportunity + heavy metal retention → systemic immune suppression. On the GI-MAP, low SIgA combined with Candida overgrowth signals this pattern.
- Confidence band: Core

*Finding 3:*
- Claim: Candida is an opportunistic organism that proliferates specifically when mucosal immune defenses are depleted or when gut microbiome disruption shifts pH.
- Evidence: Module 6 Slides 1PP (course/module-06/2025+M6+1-149+Slides+1PP.md), page 56: fungi described as opportunistic organisms that exploit disrupted microbiome ecology and depleted host defenses.
- Logic: Under healthy microbiome conditions, commensal bacteria and intact SIgA prevent Candida overgrowth through colonization resistance and immune surveillance. When SIgA falls below 510 ug/g (the GI-MAP lower reference limit) or when antibiotic use eliminates competitive bacteria, Candida proliferates unchecked. The transition from commensal to pathobiont is ecologically determined — Candida doesn't cause the dysbiosis, it exploits it. This means SIgA restoration is as important as direct antifungal intervention.
- Confidence band: Core

*Finding 4:*
- Claim: The GI-MAP Candida Suite (serum IgG/IgM/IgA antibodies to Candida antigens) provides systemic Candida immune burden assessment beyond stool DNA, improving clinical sensitivity.
- Evidence: Module 6 Slides 1PP (course/module-06/2025+M6+1-149+Slides+1PP.md), pages 69–72: Candida Suite serum antibody panel described — IgG (past/chronic exposure), IgM (acute/active infection), IgA (mucosal immune response).
- Logic: Stool DNA (GI-MAP qPCR) detects Candida organisms present in the intestinal lumen at the time of collection but can miss systemic or tissue-invasive Candida not reflected in stool. Serum antibodies (IgG, IgM, IgA) capture the immune system's active and historical response to Candida antigens systemically. IgG elevation indicates chronic or past exposure; IgM elevation indicates acute current infection; IgA elevation indicates ongoing mucosal immune activation. Combining stool PCR with Candida Suite serum antibodies provides a more complete picture of fungal burden and tissue involvement.
- Confidence band: Core

### 3. Parasitic Load
**Confidence band:** Core
**Finding count:** 4
**Completion status:** Complete

**Summary:** Parasitic pathogens detected on the GI-MAP (Blastocystis, Cryptosporidium, Entamoeba histolytica, Giardia) cause nutrient malabsorption, intestinal permeability, and mucosal immune disruption. Giardia is clinically significant for its biliary reservoir — it migrates into bile ducts, enabling reinfection after apparent clearance. Treatment requires extended courses (6–12 weeks for biofilm-forming parasites) and cannot rely on standard 7–10 day protocols. Parasites directly damage the intestinal wall, producing lesions, exudates, and bypassing protective IgE mechanisms.

**Reasoning chains:**

*Finding 1:*
- Claim: Giardia migrates into the biliary system and uses it as a reservoir, enabling reinfection after intestinal treatment and requiring extended treatment protocols.
- Evidence: Module 6 Transcript Part 02 (course/module-06/module-06-transcript-part-02.md), slide 111 area: Giardia's biliary reservoir mechanism — "Giardia is infamous for moving up into the bile ducts" and reinfecting from there after intestinal treatment.
- Logic: Standard antiparasitic treatments reach intestinal lumen concentrations sufficient to clear luminal Giardia, but bile duct concentrations of most antiparasitic agents are lower. Giardia residing in biliary tissue survives the treatment course and re-sequesters in the intestine when bile is excreted postprandially. This is why single-course Giardia treatment often fails and why follow-up GI-MAP testing is essential to confirm eradication rather than clinical symptom resolution.
- Confidence band: Core

*Finding 2:*
- Claim: Entamoeba histolytica can migrate extraintestinally to the liver (amoebic liver abscess) and other organs if not identified and treated.
- Evidence: Module 6 Transcript Part 02 (course/module-06/module-06-transcript-part-02.md), slide 110: Entamoeba histolytica's capacity for intestinal wall invasion and extraintestinal migration to liver.
- Logic: Unlike commensal Entamoeba species (E. coli, Endolimax nana — detected on GI-MAP but not pathogenic), E. histolytica is invasive. It secretes cysteine proteases that digest the extracellular matrix of the intestinal wall, enabling mucosal penetration and access to the portal circulation. Through portal blood flow, E. histolytica reaches the liver and forms amoebic abscesses. The GI-MAP's distinction between E. histolytica (pathogenic) and E. coli (non-pathogenic) is clinically essential — detecting one triggers urgent treatment while the other is informational only.
- Confidence band: Core

*Finding 3:*
- Claim: Parasites cause direct intestinal damage including lesions and exudates that disrupt mucosal integrity and nutrient absorption, exceeding the damage from dysbiotic bacteria.
- Evidence: Module 6 Slides 1PP (course/module-06/2025+M6+1-149+Slides+1PP.md), page 44: parasites described as causing direct wall damage — lesions, exudates, biofilm formation — that bacteria typically do not produce.
- Logic: Parasites have physical mechanisms of intestinal damage that bacterial dysbiosis cannot replicate: mechanical attachment (Giardia's sucking disk physically strips microvilli), cytolytic enzyme secretion (Entamoeba), and physical tissue invasion. The resulting villous damage impairs brush-border enzyme function (lactase, sucrase), reducing carbohydrate and fat absorption. This explains why parasite-positive GI-MAPs often correlate with multiple food intolerances — the intolerance is secondary to structural intestinal damage, not primary immune reactivity.
- Confidence band: Core

*Finding 4:*
- Claim: Giardia directly causes nutrient malabsorption and intestinal hyperpermeability that contributes to multi-system FDN dysfunction.
- Evidence: Module 6 Transcript Part 02 (course/module-06/module-06-transcript-part-02.md), slide 111: Giardia described as causing nutrient depletion and intestinal permeability — "when Giardia is present, we have increased intestinal permeability."
- Logic: Giardia attaches to intestinal epithelium via a ventral adhesive disk and physically disrupts tight junction proteins (occludin, claudin). This increases intestinal permeability (the GI-MAP's Zonulin marker captures this systemic permeability signal). Simultaneously, villous blunting from Giardia attachment reduces absorptive surface area, causing fat, vitamin B12, and fat-soluble vitamin malabsorption. The combination of increased permeability (antigens crossing into circulation) and nutrient depletion (reduced micronutrient availability) creates downstream FDN five-lab abnormalities — elevated HSCRP (from antigen-driven immune activation) and depleted adrenal/thyroid substrate availability.
- Confidence band: Core

### 4. Dysbiotic and Opportunistic Bacteria
**Confidence band:** Core
**Finding count:** 4
**Completion status:** Complete

**Summary:** Dysbiotic and opportunistic bacteria on the GI-MAP include organisms capable of driving autoimmune disease through molecular mimicry (Klebsiella, Citrobacter, Proteus, Prevotella), histamine production (Morganella), and systemic inflammatory signaling. The GI-MAP distinguishes four dysbiotic patterns (Insufficiency, Inflammatory, Combo, Digestive Dysfunction), each requiring different therapeutic priorities. Klebsiella is the most clinically significant autoimmune trigger, linked to ankylosing spondylitis, rheumatoid arthritis, and mercury toxicity amplification.

**Reasoning chains:**

*Finding 1:*
- Claim: Klebsiella drives autoimmune disease through molecular mimicry with HLA-B27, connecting GI dysbiosis to ankylosing spondylitis and rheumatoid arthritis.
- Evidence: Module 6 Slides 1PP (course/module-06/2025+M6+1-149+Slides+1PP.md), page 32: Klebsiella listed as the primary autoimmune trigger for ankylosing spondylitis (AS) and rheumatoid arthritis (RA) through molecular mimicry with the HLA-B27 antigen.
- Logic: Klebsiella pneumoniae surface proteins share structural homology with HLA-B27, a major histocompatibility complex antigen. When the immune system generates antibodies against Klebsiella, those antibodies cross-react with HLA-B27-expressing cells in synovial tissue and vertebral joints, producing autoimmune joint inflammation. The GI-MAP flags Klebsiella as a potential autoimmune trigger because its presence in a patient with joint symptoms or elevated ANA/inflammatory markers identifies a treatable upstream cause of the downstream autoimmune presentation.
- Confidence band: Core

*Finding 2:*
- Claim: Morganella spp. produces histamine through bacterial histidine decarboxylase, directly contributing to histamine intolerance symptoms in GI-MAP-positive patients.
- Evidence: Module 6 Slides 1PP (course/module-06/2025+M6+1-149+Slides+1PP.md), page 26: Morganella listed as a histamine-producing opportunistic bacterium — "Morganella spp. produces histamine."
- Logic: Morganella morphs encode the enzyme histidine decarboxylase, which converts dietary histidine to histamine. In a healthy gut with intact diamine oxidase (DAO) enzyme activity, dietary histamine is degraded before systemic absorption. However, when Morganella proliferates in dysbiotic conditions, luminal histamine concentration can exceed DAO's degradation capacity, leading to systemic absorption. This produces the clinical picture of histamine intolerance: flushing, urticaria, headaches, and GI cramping triggered by high-histamine foods. Treating Morganella overgrowth (and concomitantly supporting DAO) resolves the histamine excess at its source.
- Confidence band: Core

*Finding 3:*
- Claim: Multiple opportunistic bacteria (Citrobacter, Proteus, Prevotella) drive autoimmune disease through different molecular mimicry targets, expanding the GI-MAP's role in rheumatological assessment.
- Evidence: Module 6 Slides 1PP (course/module-06/2025+M6+1-149+Slides+1PP.md), pages 31–34: molecular mimicry table — Citrobacter → RA, Proteus → RA and AS, Prevotella → RA.
- Logic: Molecular mimicry is a general mechanism by which bacterial surface antigens that share structural homology with human self-antigens trigger cross-reactive autoimmune responses. Citrobacter and Proteus share epitopes with collagen and joint antigens, driving RA. Prevotella copri has been specifically identified in early-onset RA in research (Pianta et al., 2017). The GI-MAP panel's inclusion of these organisms as "Potential Autoimmune Triggers" converts a standard stool test into a rheumatological screening tool — particularly valuable when autoimmune presentations are present but serological markers (RF, anti-CCP) are negative or borderline.
- Confidence band: Core

*Finding 4:*
- Claim: The GI-MAP identifies four dysbiotic patterns (Insufficiency, Inflammatory, Combo, Digestive Dysfunction) that determine therapeutic priority sequencing.
- Evidence: Module 6 Slides 1PP (course/module-06/2025+M6+1-149+Slides+1PP.md), pages 37–41: four dysbiotic pattern definitions with clinical implications for therapeutic approach.
- Logic: The dysbiotic pattern framework converts individual organism elevations into systemic pattern recognition. Insufficiency dysbiosis (low commensals, low pathogens) calls for re-inoculation as the primary intervention. Inflammatory dysbiosis (elevated opportunistic bacteria, elevated markers) calls for removal first. Combo dysbiosis (both patterns simultaneously) requires sequential approach — remove pathogens, then rebuild commensals. Digestive dysfunction dysbiosis (low Elastase-1, high fat in stool) requires enzyme replacement as the foundational step before any microbiome intervention, because undigested food substrates perpetuate bacterial overgrowth regardless of antimicrobial treatment.
- Confidence band: Core

### 5. Commensal Flora Balance
**Confidence band:** Core
**Finding count:** 4
**Completion status:** Complete

**Summary:** Commensal flora (Bacteroides fragilis, Bifidobacterium, Lactobacillus, Akkermansia, Faecalibacterium prausnitzii, Roseburia) provide essential functions including SCFAs, immune education, colonization resistance, and mucosal integrity. The Firmicutes:Bacteroidetes (F:B) ratio is the macro-level diversity indicator — elevated Firmicutes correlates with metabolic dysfunction and obesity. Roseburia is the primary butyrate producer and its depletion is associated with leaky gut and colonic inflammation. Commensal function assessment requires interpreting both individual organisms and their collective ecological balance.

**Reasoning chains:**

*Finding 1:*
- Claim: Commensal flora perform essential host-protective functions including SCFA production, immune tolerance education, and colonization resistance against pathogens.
- Evidence: Module 6 Slides 1PP (course/module-06/2025+M6+1-149+Slides+1PP.md), pages 14 and 21: commensal flora functions listed — "produce SCFAs, educate the immune system, and resist colonization by pathogens."
- Logic: Short-chain fatty acids (acetate, propionate, butyrate) produced by commensal fermentation of dietary fiber serve as primary energy substrates for colonocytes (butyrate), regulate hepatic glucose metabolism (propionate), and modulate peripheral immune cell function (acetate). Immune education via commensal exposure is the mechanistic basis of the "hygiene hypothesis" — commensal diversity shapes Th1/Th2/Treg balance and prevents inappropriate inflammatory responses. Colonization resistance means commensal occupancy of intestinal niches physically prevents pathogen attachment — which is why antibiotic-induced depletion dramatically increases susceptibility to C. difficile and other opportunistic infections.
- Confidence band: Core

*Finding 2:*
- Claim: Roseburia is the primary butyrate-producing commensal and its depletion is directly associated with intestinal permeability and colonic inflammatory disease.
- Evidence: Module 6 Transcript Part 02 (course/module-06/module-06-transcript-part-02.md), GI MAP Report addendum: Roseburia described as a key butyrate producer — "Roseburia is one of the most important butyrate-producing bacteria in the human colon."
- Logic: Butyrate is the preferred energy substrate for colonocytes and is essential for maintaining tight junction protein expression (particularly occludin and ZO-1). When Roseburia is depleted, colonocyte butyrate supply falls, tight junction integrity weakens, and intestinal permeability increases. This creates the physiological basis for elevated Zonulin on the GI-MAP — the permeability marker — and explains why isolated probiotic supplementation with Lactobacillus or Bifidobacterium fails to restore butyrate levels (they are not butyrate producers). Dietary substrate restoration (resistant starch, inulin) is required to restore Roseburia.
- Confidence band: Core

*Finding 3:*
- Claim: The Firmicutes:Bacteroidetes ratio is the GI-MAP's macroecological diversity indicator — elevated Firmicutes relative to Bacteroidetes correlates with metabolic syndrome, obesity, and systemic inflammation.
- Evidence: Module 6 Slides 1PP (course/module-06/2025+M6+1-149+Slides+1PP.md), page 22: F:B ratio reference range interpretation — "normal F:B ratio is <1.00" and elevated ratio correlates with metabolic dysfunction.
- Logic: Firmicutes include highly efficient energy-harvesting bacteria (Clostridiales) that extract more calories from equivalent food substrates than Bacteroidetes-dominant microbiomes. A high F:B ratio means excess energy extraction from diet, contributing to adipogenesis and metabolic syndrome. The GI-MAP reference range (<1.00 for F:B) captures this at the phylum level — though individual species within each phylum can be pathogenic or beneficial. The F:B ratio is an ecological summary metric that contextualizes individual organism findings.
- Confidence band: Core

*Finding 4:*
- Claim: Bacteroides fragilis serves dual roles as a commensal (producing polysaccharide A that promotes Treg tolerance) and potential pathobiont when elevated beyond normal range.
- Evidence: Module 6 Slides 1PP (course/module-06/2025+M6+1-149+Slides+1PP.md), page 16: B. fragilis described as a beneficial commensal with immune-modulating properties including Treg promotion.
- Logic: Bacteroides fragilis produces polysaccharide A (PSA), which is recognized by TLR2 on Treg cells and promotes IL-10-mediated immune tolerance, suppressing Th17-driven inflammation. At normal reference range levels (1.60e9–2.50e11), B. fragilis contributes to systemic immune balance. However, the GI-MAP's reference range is essential — values exceeding the upper limit signal pathobiont transition, where B. fragilis's enterotoxin production (ETBF — enterotoxigenic B. fragilis) can drive colonic inflammation and has been linked to colorectal cancer risk. The same organism is beneficial at low levels and harmful at high levels.
- Confidence band: Core

### 6. Calprotectin and Lactoferrin
**Confidence band:** Core
**Finding count:** 4
**Completion status:** Complete

**Summary:** Calprotectin is the GI-MAP's primary intestinal inflammation marker, released by neutrophils during active mucosal inflammation with 93–95% sensitivity for distinguishing inflammatory bowel disease from IBS. Lactoferrin is a secondary iron-binding protein marker also released by neutrophils, providing confirmatory validation of calprotectin findings. Both markers are elevated by non-pathological confounders (NSAIDs, smoking, obesity) that must be excluded before clinical interpretation. The combination of calprotectin + lactoferrin is more diagnostically specific than either alone.

**Reasoning chains:**

*Finding 1:*
- Claim: Calprotectin has 93–95% sensitivity for distinguishing inflammatory bowel disease from irritable bowel syndrome, making it a critical GI triage marker.
- Evidence: Module 6 Transcript Part 02 (course/module-06/module-06-transcript-part-02.md), slide 121 area: calprotectin described as having "93–95% sensitivity for IBD vs IBS differentiation."
- Logic: Calprotectin is a calcium- and zinc-binding protein released from neutrophil cytoplasm during active mucosal inflammation. IBS is a functional disorder without neutrophilic infiltration; IBD (Crohn's, ulcerative colitis) is a structural inflammatory condition with robust neutrophil activation. The near-100% sensitivity means that a negative calprotectin effectively rules out active IBD — a clinically valuable negative predictive value. A positive calprotectin triggers further gastroenterological investigation (colonoscopy, imaging) rather than functional GI intervention.
- Confidence band: Core

*Finding 2:*
- Claim: Non-pathological confounders (NSAIDs, smoking, obesity) elevate calprotectin independently of intestinal disease and must be excluded before clinical interpretation.
- Evidence: Module 6 Transcript Part 02 (course/module-06/module-06-transcript-part-02.md), confounder discussion for calprotectin: NSAIDs, smoking, and obesity listed as false-positive drivers.
- Logic: NSAIDs inhibit cyclooxygenase enzymes in intestinal epithelium, producing direct mucosal irritation and neutrophilic infiltration that elevates calprotectin without underlying IBD. Smoking increases luminal oxidative stress and mucosal immune activation. Obesity produces systemic low-grade inflammation with some intestinal neutrophil activity. In any calprotectin-positive result, confounder exclusion is the first clinical step — a patient using ibuprofen daily could have a calprotectin of 200 ug/g purely from NSAID use, not IBD. Removing the confounder and re-testing provides the true signal.
- Confidence band: Core

*Finding 3:*
- Claim: Lactoferrin is a complementary neutrophil-released iron-binding glycoprotein that confirms calprotectin findings and adds specificity to intestinal inflammation assessment.
- Evidence: Module 6 Transcript Part 02 (course/module-06/module-06-transcript-part-02.md), lactoferrin discussion — "a secondary marker to calprotectin, providing confirmatory evidence of neutrophilic mucosal inflammation."
- Logic: Lactoferrin and calprotectin are both released from neutrophil secondary granules during mucosal inflammation, but they have different molecular properties and clinical sensitivities. Calprotectin at 93–95% sensitivity is the primary screen. Lactoferrin at ~2% additional diagnostic specificity validates calprotectin findings, particularly in borderline-elevated calprotectin cases (100–200 ug/g where clinical ambiguity exists). When both are elevated simultaneously, the probability of active intestinal neutrophilic inflammation is very high, warranting urgent referral.
- Confidence band: Core

*Finding 4:*
- Claim: Calprotectin findings in GI-MAP case studies show how the marker integrates with other panel results to determine therapeutic priority and referral necessity.
- Evidence: GI-MAP case study data (Joan GI-MAP results_case study.md and Ashley S GI MAP results.md): calprotectin values and their context within the full panel — Joan's calprotectin 91 ug/g (within reference <173), Ashley's values.
- Logic: In Joan's GI-MAP (calprotectin 91, normal reference <173), the absence of significant calprotectin elevation despite high beta-glucuronidase (3584) and low SIgA (312) indicates that mucosal inflammation is not yet at the neutrophilic infiltration stage — the dysbiosis is metabolically and immunologically significant but not yet producing structural intestinal inflammation detectable by calprotectin. This pattern prioritizes the beta-glucuronidase and SIgA interventions over inflammation suppression, demonstrating how calprotectin absence focuses therapeutic sequencing toward root causes rather than inflammatory downstream effects.
- Confidence band: Core

### 7. Beta-glucuronidase
**Confidence band:** Core
**Finding count:** 4
**Completion status:** Complete

**Summary:** Beta-glucuronidase is a bacterial enzyme produced by dysbiotic organisms (Bacteroides, E. coli, Staphylococcus) that cleaves glucuronic acid from Phase 2 liver conjugates, deconjugating estrogens and other toxins that were prepared for excretion. Elevated beta-glucuronidase creates enterohepatic recirculation of estrogens, driving estrogen dominance symptoms and disrupting the liver's Phase 2 detoxification capacity. It creates a bidirectional feedback loop with liver function — elevated beta-glucuronidase increases hepatic load, and impaired hepatic conjugation allows more substrate for beta-glucuronidase deconjugation. Reference range upper limit is 2486 U/mL; values above this indicate clinically significant estrogen recirculation risk.

**Reasoning chains:**

*Finding 1:*
- Claim: Beta-glucuronidase disrupts Phase 2 liver glucuronidation by deconjugating glucuronide-conjugated metabolites (estrogens, bilirubin, bisphenols) in the intestine, forcing the liver to re-conjugate them and increasing hepatic metabolic burden.
- Evidence: Module 6 Transcript Part 02 (course/module-06/module-06-transcript-part-02.md), slide 119 area: beta-glucuronidase described as an enzyme that "cleaves the glucuronic acid bond, releasing free estrogens and other toxins back into circulation."
- Logic: The liver's Phase 2 glucuronidation pathway conjugates estrogens (estradiol, estrone, estriol) and xenoestrogens with glucuronic acid, rendering them water-soluble for biliary excretion. These conjugates are released into the intestinal lumen via bile. When beta-glucuronidase is elevated (produced by dysbiotic E. coli, Bacteroides, Staphylococcus), the enzyme cleaves the glucuronic acid bond in the intestine. The freed estrogens are reabsorbed through intestinal enterocytes into portal circulation and return to the liver — enterohepatic recirculation. This forces the liver to re-conjugate the same estrogen molecule repeatedly, depleting glucuronyl transferase enzyme capacity and UDP-glucuronic acid substrate while prolonging estrogen's biological half-life.
- Confidence band: Core

*Finding 2:*
- Claim: Elevated beta-glucuronidase drives estrogen dominance by extending estrogen biological half-life and recirculating estrogens that should have been excreted in feces.
- Evidence: Module 6 Transcript Part 02 (course/module-06/module-06-transcript-part-02.md), slide 119: estrogen recirculation consequences — elevated beta-glucuronidase is directly connected to estrogen dominance symptoms including PMS, heavy periods, fibrocystic breasts, and hormone-sensitive cancer risk.
- Logic: Estrogens normally have a regulated excretory cycle: hepatic conjugation → biliary excretion → intestinal clearance in feces. Beta-glucuronidase interrupts fecal elimination, returning bioactive estrogens to circulation. Elevated circulating estrogens competitively bind estrogen receptors (ERα, ERβ) in breast, uterine, and ovarian tissue, producing estrogen-dominant symptoms. This connects the GI-MAP's beta-glucuronidase finding to the DUTCH comprehensive hormone panel — a core FDN five-lab bidirectional link: gut dysbiosis → hormone panel abnormalities.
- Confidence band: Core

*Finding 3:*
- Claim: The organisms responsible for elevated beta-glucuronidase are identifiable on the GI-MAP, enabling targeted antimicrobial intervention rather than non-specific treatment.
- Evidence: Module 6 Transcript Part 02 (course/module-06/module-06-transcript-part-02.md), slide 119: beta-glucuronidase producers identified on GI-MAP — "E. coli, Bacteroides fragilis, and Staphylococcus are the primary producers of beta-glucuronidase."
- Logic: Because the GI-MAP quantifies the specific organisms producing beta-glucuronidase, a targeted clinical response is possible: if E. coli is the primary driver, E. coli-targeted antimicrobials (berberine, oregano oil) are prioritized; if Bacteroides fragilis is elevated into the pathobiont range, Bacteroides-targeted treatment is selected. This specificity converts the beta-glucuronidase marker from a non-specific "gut health problem" indicator into an actionable therapeutic target list. Calcium-D-glucarate supplementation (a beta-glucuronidase inhibitor) can provide adjunctive support regardless of which organisms are driving the elevation.
- Confidence band: Core

*Finding 4:*
- Claim: Beta-glucuronidase elevation creates a bidirectional feedback loop with liver function — elevated enzyme increases hepatic load, and impaired liver Phase 2 capacity produces more substrate for beta-glucuronidase to act on.
- Evidence: Module 6 Transcript Part 02 (course/module-06/module-06-transcript-part-02.md), slide 119: the gut-liver axis connection — beta-glucuronidase elevation forces liver to work harder, and a liver already impaired by toxin load produces bile with higher conjugate concentrations, providing more substrate for the enzyme.
- Logic: Phase 2 glucuronidation impairment (from toxin load, nutritional deficiencies in UGT enzyme cofactors, or genetic UGT1A polymorphisms) means unconjugated metabolites accumulate in bile or recirculate in blood at higher concentrations. These unconjugated metabolites that do reach the gut are partially conjugated — higher substrate availability for beta-glucuronidase to act on. Simultaneously, the glucuronide conjugates that do reach the gut get deconjugated by beta-glucuronidase and return to the liver, increasing the liver's conjugation workload. The two systems amplify each other's dysfunction in a positive feedback loop, making joint gut-liver intervention more effective than treating either system in isolation.
- Confidence band: Core

### 8. Anti-gliadin IgA
**Confidence band:** Core
**Finding count:** 4
**Completion status:** Complete

**Summary:** Anti-gliadin IgA (AGA) on the GI-MAP measures the local intestinal IgA antibody response to gliadin (wheat gluten protein), indicating mucosal immune reactivity to gluten. AGA is distinct from the celiac-specific tissue transglutaminase antibodies (tTG-IgA) measured in serum; a positive AGA can occur in non-celiac gluten sensitivity (NCGS) without a true celiac diagnosis. A critical clinical dependency exists: SIgA must be adequate for AGA to be interpretable — low SIgA suppresses the AGA response, producing false negatives that mask genuine gluten reactivity.

**Reasoning chains:**

*Finding 1:*

### 9. Secretory IgA — GI tract
**Confidence band:** Core
**Finding count:** 4
**Completion status:** Complete

**Summary:** Secretory IgA (SIgA) is the primary first-line mucosal defense protein of the gastrointestinal tract, produced by plasma cells in gut-associated lymphoid tissue (GALT) and secreted into the intestinal lumen. SIgA levels on the GI-MAP range from 510–2010 ug/g; values below 510 indicate partial immune suppression, and values below 200 ug/g indicate total mucosal immune shutdown. The HPA axis is the primary systemic regulator of SIgA — cortisol from adrenal dysregulation is the most common clinical driver of low SIgA, creating a gut-HPA bidirectional link that makes adrenal support a prerequisite for SIgA restoration.

**Reasoning chains:**

*Finding 1:*
- Claim: Secretory IgA is the intestinal tract's primary first-line defense, produced locally in GALT and secreted into the lumen to neutralize pathogens before mucosal contact.
- Evidence: Module 6 Transcript Part 02 (course/module-06/module-06-transcript-part-02.md), slide 117: SIgA described as "the first-line defense of the mucosal immune system, produced in the gut-associated lymphoid tissue."
- Logic: Unlike serum immunoglobulins (IgG, IgM), which circulate systemically and require antigen entry into blood for activation, SIgA operates exclusively at mucosal surfaces. Its dimeric structure (two IgA monomers + secretory component) resists proteolytic degradation in the intestinal lumen. SIgA neutralizes pathogens, toxins, and food antigens by immune exclusion — binding them in the lumen and preventing attachment to epithelial receptors. This pre-contact neutralization is fundamentally different from the systemic immune response and is the primary reason gut health cannot be assessed by serum immune markers alone.
- Confidence band: Core

*Finding 2:*
- Claim: SIgA below 200 ug/g represents total mucosal immune shutdown — at this level, the intestinal immune system cannot mount meaningful defense against any pathogen or antigen.
- Evidence: Module 6 Transcript Part 02 (course/module-06/module-06-transcript-part-02.md), slide 117: clinical threshold interpretation — "below 200 ug/g is considered a state of total mucosal immune suppression."
- Logic: The GI-MAP reference range for SIgA is 510–2010 ug/g. Values 200–510 indicate partial suppression — some mucosal immune function remains but is significantly impaired. Values below 200 ug/g indicate the GALT plasma cell population has collapsed to the point where meaningful luminal IgA production is absent. At this level, every food antigen and pathogen the patient ingests crosses the mucosal barrier without immune surveillance. This is the physiological state that explains why severely stressed patients develop multiple food intolerances simultaneously — not because they are suddenly eating reactive foods, but because mucosal immune exclusion has failed.
- Confidence band: Core

*Finding 3:*
- Claim: Chronic stress and HPA axis dysregulation are the primary systemic drivers of low SIgA — cortisol directly suppresses GALT plasma cell IgA production.
- Evidence: Module 6 Transcript Part 02 (course/module-06/module-06-transcript-part-02.md), slides 101–102: SIgA-stress connection — "SIgA levels can be severely impacted by chronic stress. Low levels are associated with chronic dysbiosis, chronic exposure to food antigens, chronic stress, immune-compromised clients, protein malnutrition."
- Logic: Cortisol's glucocorticoid receptor actions in mucosal tissue downregulate NF-κB-mediated inflammatory and immune signaling in GALT plasma cells. Chronically elevated cortisol (as measured by DUTCH cortisol awakening response and diurnal cortisol pattern) inhibits IL-6 and IL-10 signaling necessary for IgA class-switching in B cells. The result is progressive depletion of mucosal IgA production capacity. This creates the cortisol-SIgA bidirectional feedback loop: adrenal dysfunction → low SIgA → pathogen colonization → chronic GI inflammation → HPA re-activation → sustained cortisol elevation and continued SIgA depletion.
- Confidence band: Core

*Finding 4:*
- Claim: Cortisol-mediated SIgA suppression creates a self-reinforcing feedback loop — elevated cortisol reduces mucosal immune defense, permitting pathogen colonization, which sustains inflammatory HPA activation, which sustains cortisol elevation and continued SIgA suppression.
- Evidence: Module 6 Transcript Part 02 (course/module-06/module-06-transcript-part-02.md), lines 101–102: "SIgA levels can be severely impacted by chronic stress. Low levels are associated with chronic dysbiosis, chronic exposure to food antigens, chronic stress, immune-compromised clients, protein malnutrition."
- Logic: Cortisol's glucocorticoid receptor actions in mucosal tissue downregulate secretory IgA production (inhibition of NF-κB in GALT plasma cells; reduced IL-6/IL-10 for IgA class-switching). Depleted luminal SIgA removes the first-line defense against pathogen colonization. Colonized pathogens produce LPS, which activates TLR4, triggers NFκB-mediated cytokine production, and independently re-activates the HPA axis. The result is a self-sustaining loop that will not resolve until either HPA activation is reduced or pathogen burden is cleared — identifying the entry point requires the full five-lab FDN panel.

### 10. Occult Blood
**Confidence band:** Core
**Finding count:** 3
**Completion status:** Complete

**Summary:** Fecal immunochemical test (FIT) for occult blood on the GI-MAP measures hidden blood in stool using antibodies specific to human hemoglobin, distinguishing it from dietary blood sources. The clinical threshold is <10 ug/g; values 5–9 are sub-threshold but clinically noteworthy; values 10–50 warrant medical investigation; values >50 require urgent gastroenterological referral. Occult blood serves as the GI-MAP's safety gate — a positive result overrides all other clinical priorities and triggers immediate conventional medical referral regardless of other panel findings.

**Reasoning chains:**

*Finding 1:*
- Claim: FIT occult blood at ≥10 ug/g requires urgent medical referral — this marker overrides all other GI-MAP findings and is a patient safety threshold, not a functional medicine optimization target.
- Evidence: Module 6 Transcript Part 02 (course/module-06/module-06-transcript-part-02.md), slide 120 area: referral threshold — "if occult blood is 10 or above, this patient needs to be referred to their medical doctor immediately."
- Logic: Occult blood in stool indicates active bleeding somewhere in the gastrointestinal tract. While the bleeding source can be benign (hemorrhoids, fissures, polyps), it can also be colorectal cancer, upper GI malignancy, or serious intestinal pathology. The FIT test uses antibodies specific to human hemoglobin — it does not react to dietary meat blood, eliminating dietary false positives. A ≥10 ug/g result in a clinical FDN context is not a functional medicine finding to address with probiotics or herbs — it is a medical safety threshold requiring colonoscopy or upper endoscopy investigation before any other intervention proceeds.
- Confidence band: Core

*Finding 2:*
- Claim: Occult blood values are stratified clinically — 5–9 ug/g is sub-threshold but warrants monitoring; 10–50 warrants medical investigation; >50 requires urgent gastroenterological referral.
- Evidence: Module 6 Transcript Part 02 (course/module-06/module-06-transcript-part-02.md), slide 120: occult blood stratification levels — "10–50: refer to medical doctor; >50: urgent referral."
- Logic: The stratification reflects the probability of serious pathology at different blood concentrations. Sub-threshold (5–9) values may reflect minor mucosal irritation (from NSAIDs, constipation, or minor hemorrhoids) that warrants dietary and lifestyle intervention but not immediate medical referral. Values 10–50 represent sustained or moderate bleeding requiring diagnostic workup (colonoscopy or upper GI endoscopy) but not emergency medical evaluation. Values >50 suggest active significant bleeding from a source that requires urgent identification — this level may indicate active ulceration, polyps with high bleeding potential, or early-stage malignancy with ulcerated surface.
- Confidence band: Core

*Finding 3:*
- Claim: The FIT test is human-hemoglobin specific, eliminating dietary false positives from red meat consumption — a clinical advantage over older guaiac-based stool blood tests.
- Evidence: Module 6 Transcript Part 02 (course/module-06/module-06-transcript-part-02.md), slide 120: FIT specificity for human hemoglobin — the immunochemical method distinguishes human from animal hemoglobin.
- Logic: The older guaiac-based fecal occult blood test (gFOBT) detects heme peroxidase activity, which is present in both human and dietary animal hemoglobin. Patients must avoid red meat for 3 days before testing to prevent false positives. The FIT test uses monoclonal antibodies that recognize epitopes on human globin chains that differ structurally from bovine or porcine globin. This antibody specificity means FIT results are not confounded by dietary meat consumption — a positive result on a FIT test reflects human intestinal bleeding regardless of recent diet, making it clinically more reliable and eliminating a key pre-test protocol burden for patients.
- Confidence band: Core
